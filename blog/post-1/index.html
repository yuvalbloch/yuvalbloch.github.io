The Unseen Architects: Exploring the Fundamental Properties of Life

Before we dive into the fundamental principles that shape life, let’s take a moment to observe. If you’re outside—or can step out—lie under a tree and look up into its intricate canopy. Or examine the fine structure of a flower, a bustling ant colony, or even the fractal spirals of a broccoli or cauliflower floret in your fridge. If none of these are handy, look closely at your fingerprint.

Spend a few minutes in quiet observation. What patterns do you see? How do they form? How does each branch of a tree “know” where to grow? How does an ant find its way? How does every skin cell contribute to the unique loops and whorls of your fingerprint?

These questions lead us to today’s exploration: the subtle, awe-inspiring properties that define living systems.

A Simple Exercise in Complexity

To begin, let’s try something hands-on. Grab some grid paper or use this interactive emulator: discover-automata.vercel.app

Set the number of states to 2 and start with a single filled square at the top.

Rule 2: The Predictable Path

Use this rule: in each new row, fill in the square directly beneath a square that was filled in the previous row and to its left (Rule 2 in the emulator).

After a few rows, you’ll see a simple diagonal line. The pattern is mechanical, even dull. It follows a strict, deterministic rule, and its future is entirely predictable.

But life rarely behaves so neatly.

Rule 90: Stepping Into Complexity

Now, let’s try something a little more interesting.

Start again with one filled square at the top. This time, follow Rule 90: a square becomes filled if either—but not both—of its left or right neighbors in the previous row is filled.

After 10 rows, you’ll notice a striking triangular pattern made up of smaller, repeating triangles. Continue to 20 rows, and the pattern grows in intricacy—fractals within fractals.

Here’s the fascinating part: each square only “knows” about its two neighbors. No global plan, no central control. Yet from that minimal interaction emerges rich complexity.

Self-Organization & Fractals: Nature’s Quiet Blueprint

Now, think back to the tree, the flower, the ant colony, or your fingerprint. None of these have a central conductor orchestrating every detail. Instead, each part follows a few local rules, responding to its environment. This phenomenon—where order emerges from the bottom up—is called self-organization.

You might also have noticed something else: scaling. A tree branch resembles the whole tree. A fern’s leaf mimics the plant’s form. These are fractals, patterns that repeat at different scales. Fractals are more than mathematical curiosities—they are fundamental to how life builds itself.

Take a moment to appreciate what you’ve just created: using nothing but simple rules and a grid, you’ve simulated some of life’s essential properties—complexity, self-organization, and scale.

The Edge of Predictability: Life’s Beautiful Chaos

Ready for the next step?

Try Rule 30 in the emulator—a rule famous for producing surprisingly unpredictable behavior. The rule is simple: a square becomes filled if the three squares above it match one of these patterns:

  • Black, White, White
  • White, Black, White
  • White, Black, Black
  • White, White, Black

Start with one filled square and let the pattern evolve for many rows. At first, you may see repeating motifs—but then chaos emerges. Try to predict the 1000th row.

You can’t. No one can. Mathematically, this system is deterministic yet unpredictable—a classic example of emergent complexity.

Even though the rules never change, and you know the initial state, the only way to know what happens 1000 rows later is to build the pattern row by row.

A tiny change in the starting condition can lead to dramatically different outcomes. Sound familiar? Biological systems are much the same.

Life at the Edge of Chaos

Just like cellular automata, living systems span a spectrum—from predictable patterns to unpredictable chaos. Even with deep knowledge of the rules and current state, the future may remain unknowable. And this unpredictability is not a flaw—it’s a feature of life.

Consider this: you might never have been born if your father hadn’t gone to a particular supply store to buy a pen—where he met your mother. Life’s twists and turns hinge on fragile, often unpredictable chains of events.

At first glance, it’s frightening that no matter how wise we are, we cannot perfectly predict the outcome of our actions. It’s scary enough to become the central motif in almost every mythology.

Even Legends Couldn’t Predict the Future

  • The Buddha’s father hid him from pain so he would not become a spiritual leader—but that protection led him to leave everything in search of truth.
  • In Norse myth, Baldr’s mother made everything promise not to harm him—except mistletoe. That one small oversight led to his death, thanks to Loki.
  • In Antigone, just as people praise human brilliance in the Ode to Man, tragedy begins to unravel—showing that wisdom alone can’t control fate.

The Freedom of the Unpredictable

Yet unpredictability also brings freedom.

As Elphaba sings in Wicked:

“I’m through accepting limits
‘Cause someone says they’re so.
Some things I cannot change,
But ’til I try, I’ll never know.”

Not everything is possible—but far more is than we often dare to imagine. Many things we assume are impossible simply remain unknown. Accepting the unknowability of the future opens the door to trying, failing, learning, and sometimes—astonishingly—succeeding.

Unpredictability also invites self-compassion. When your efforts don’t yield the expected results, it’s not because you are broken. It’s because you’re living within a complex system where not every cause has a predictable effect.

This doesn’t mean we abandon effort or wisdom. It means we embrace the truth: even our most careful choices can lead to surprising outcomes. And within that uncertainty lies the extraordinary beauty—and potential—of life.

A Final Note: Love Beyond Chemistry

Let’s revisit a memorable exchange from The Big Bang Theory:

Raj: “Are you saying that you don’t believe two people fall in love?”
Ruchi: “Of course they do. It’s just that what people call ’love’ is actually a series of biochemical reactions in the brain that fade over time.”
Raj: “Yes. Like the old song, ‘When a Man Has a Biochemical Reaction for a Woman.’”
— Season 11, Episode 7: The Geology Methodology

At first glance, Ruchi’s perspective seems grounded in scientific reasoning. However, when we consider love through the lens of complex systems theory, a richer picture emerges. While love may originate from chemical processes in the brain, it becomes something more profound through interactions with our environment, experiences, and relationships.

In complex systems, the whole often exhibits properties that cannot be predicted solely by understanding its parts. Love, in this context, becomes something new—a real, emergent phenomenon that cannot be reduced to molecules alone.

This perspective aligns with what we know in our hearts: love is real, not merely a fleeting chemical response.
